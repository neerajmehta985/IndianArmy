Tales of valor, sacrifice and victory

1971 Indo-Pak War: The Birth of Bangladesh

December 3-16, 1971

The Indo-Pakistani War of 1971 was a decisive victory for India that resulted in the creation of Bangladesh. The war began with Operation Chengiz Khan, Pakistan's preemptive air strikes on 11 Indian airbases.

India responded with a coordinated military, diplomatic and political strategy. The Indian Army, supported by the Air Force and Navy, launched a three-pronged assault on East Pakistan (now Bangladesh).

Key battles included the Battle of Longewala where 120 Indian soldiers held off 2000 Pakistani troops, and the Battle of Hilli which was one of the bloodiest battles of the war.

After just 13 days of fighting, Pakistan surrendered on December 16, 1971, with 93,000 Pakistani troops surrendering - the largest military surrender since World War II.

"In this war, the Indian Army fought with exemplary courage and determination. The victory was not just military but moral." - Field Marshal Sam Manekshaw

Kargil War 1999: Operation Vijay

May-July 1999

The Kargil War was an armed conflict between India and Pakistan that took place in the Kargil district of Kashmir along the Line of Control (LOC). Pakistani soldiers and Kashmiri militants had infiltrated into positions on the Indian side of the LOC.

India responded with Operation Vijay, mobilizing 200,000 troops to evict the infiltrators. The Indian Air Force launched Operation Safed Sagar in support.

Key battles included the capture of Tiger Hill and Tololing. Captain Vikram Batra's famous words "Yeh dil maange more!" became the rallying cry for Indian soldiers.

After 60 days of intense fighting at altitudes over 18,000 feet, India regained control of all territories. The war resulted in 527 Indian soldiers martyred and over 1,300 wounded.

"We will fight to the last man, last bullet." - Motto of Indian Army during Kargil War

Operation Meghdoot: Siachen Glacier

April 13, 1984 - Present

Operation Meghdoot was the codename for the Indian Armed Forces' operation to capture the Siachen Glacier in the disputed Kashmir region, preempting Pakistan's Operation Ababeel.

On April 13, 1984, the Indian Army and Air Force launched the operation, airlifting soldiers to the glacier. The Kumaon Regiment was the first to land on the glacier.

Siachen is the world's highest battlefield, with temperatures dropping to -60Â°C. Despite the extreme conditions, Indian soldiers have maintained control of the glacier for over 35 years.

The operation was a success, giving India control over the entire Siachen Glacier and its tributaries. India currently holds all of the 70 km long Siachen Glacier and all of its tributary glaciers.

"Not a blade of grass grows here, yet we stand firm." - Siachen Warrior's Motto
